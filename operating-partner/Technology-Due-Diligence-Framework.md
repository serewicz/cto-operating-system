# Technology Due Diligence Framework

## Purpose

Provide a board-ready framework for evaluating whether a company's technology, team, architecture, security posture, AI readiness, and operating model can support the investment thesis.

## When to Use

- Growth equity diligence
- Acquisition diligence
- Post-close technology review
- Board-level technology risk assessment
- CEO or operating partner review of technical readiness

## Inputs

- Executive summary and management presentation
- Product and technology roadmap
- Architecture diagrams and system inventory
- Cloud, Kubernetes, infrastructure, and observability documentation
- Security policies, access reviews, vulnerability reports, and incident history
- Engineering org chart, role descriptions, and hiring plan
- Delivery process, release notes, sprint or roadmap artifacts
- Vendor list, contracts, major SaaS dependencies, and cloud spend detail
- Data architecture, data governance, and AI use case inventory
- Technical debt backlog and modernization plans
- Interviews with CEO, CTO, VP Engineering, product, security, finance, and key technical staff

## Assessment Categories

| Category | Assessment Focus | Board Relevance |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Architecture | System design, service boundaries, integrations, scalability, resilience | Determines whether the platform can support growth, integration, and product change |
| Infrastructure | Cloud posture, Kubernetes maturity, environments, observability, backups, incident response | Shows operational maturity and outage exposure |
| Security | Identity, privileged access, vulnerability management, secrets, logging, compliance readiness | Connects technology controls to customer trust, regulatory exposure, and enterprise readiness |
| Engineering Organization | Team structure, leadership capacity, role clarity, delivery ownership, hiring gaps | Indicates whether the organization can execute without founder or hero dependency |
| Delivery Process | Planning, release management, testing, CI/CD, dependency management, predictability | Determines whether roadmap commitments are credible |
| Data and AI Readiness | Data quality, governance, model usage, privacy, evaluation, human oversight | Shows whether AI initiatives can scale responsibly |
| Vendor and Open Source Risk | Critical vendors, contract exposure, license risk, dependency health | Identifies cost, continuity, legal, and integration exposure |
| Technical Debt | Legacy systems, brittle processes, manual operations, architecture debt | Explains drag on speed, margin, quality, and resilience |
| Operational Risk | Incidents, on-call model, runbooks, disaster recovery, support load | Shows whether the company can operate reliably under growth |
| Business Alignment | Product strategy, revenue objectives, customer commitments, investment priorities | Tests whether technology priorities support the deal thesis |

## Evidence Standards

Use direct evidence whenever possible. A diligence finding should cite the artifact, interview, system, or operating record that supports it.

### Strong Evidence

- Multiple artifacts support the same conclusion.
- Evidence spans more than one function or document.
- The finding is confirmed by system records, architecture artifacts, incident history, security reports, or delivery data.
- Management can explain ownership, mitigation, and tradeoffs.

### Moderate Evidence

- One or two direct artifacts support the finding.
- Interviews are consistent, but system-level evidence is incomplete.
- The issue appears real but impact or scope requires further validation.

### Weak Evidence

- Evidence is inferred from gaps, absence of documentation, or inconsistent interviews.
- The finding should be framed as a concern or validation item, not a confirmed material risk.

## Outputs

- Executive technology diligence summary
- Red/yellow/green technology risk heatmap
- Category-level findings with business impact
- Key-person risk assessment
- Security and AI governance observations
- Post-close or post-investment 100-day technology priorities
- Management questions
- Board discussion points
- Evidence-backed citations or source references

## Management Questions

- Which technology risks could materially affect the investment thesis?
- Which systems or processes depend on one person?
- What security controls are not yet appropriate for the company's customer, regulatory, or enterprise profile?
- What modernization work is required to scale revenue, integrations, or product velocity?
- Which roadmap commitments are at risk because of architecture, team capacity, or technical debt?
- What AI use cases exist today, and how are model choice, data sensitivity, and human review governed?
- What should be completed in the first 30, 60, and 100 days after investment or close?

## Board Discussion Points

- Whether technology risk is acceptable, remediable, or thesis-altering
- Which risks require purchase agreement consideration, post-close investment, or board monitoring
- Whether leadership capacity is sufficient for the next stage
- Whether AI initiatives create strategic advantage, governance risk, or both
- Whether platform modernization should be funded as growth enablement, risk reduction, or integration readiness

## Expected Outcome

A diligence output that helps investors and executives understand technology posture, material risk, ownership, required decisions, and the operating plan needed to support the business thesis.
